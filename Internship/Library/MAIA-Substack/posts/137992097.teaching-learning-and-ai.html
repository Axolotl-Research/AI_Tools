BE CHAOTIC & CONFUSED

Managing a team of researchers, I am always thinking about how to teach and learn. Researching is applied learning, and managing researchers combines structure, motivation and differentiated teaching.

In the clip below Feynman talks about the best way to teach and then contradicts himself saying he doesn’t know with a smile. He does know. The smile tells you that. But the master teacher doesn’t answer the questions, he just offers his students different approaches to the problem and invites them to the hunt for solutions.

Feynman’s advice is to be chaotic and confused. Learning as an adventure. The technique has many benefits — but one Feynman doesn’t mention is that the world tends to present itself is in a chaotic and confused fashion. In teaching that way, a teacher is preparing the student for the world — which doesn’t organize information for us.

MORE FROM LESS

As researchers, we are always students. As generalist researchers, we are always learning from experts and accustomed to being the least informed person in a room on a topic. As I like to say, if I’m the smartest guy in the room - I’m in the wrong room.

As generalists we never really become experts on any single area. Our task is to find a set of key experts or leaders in an area and learn from and listen to them. I have gotten into the habit of curating video of experts in Reduct. After 30 years researching businesses I like being able to offer direct testimony from experts in their own voice rather than my hearsay testimony of what I thought they said or meant. Quality research is curated not created.

A tool like Reduct applied to a database like YouTube improves my research product by removing me from the product. My research is more accurate with less of me directly in it.

There is another aspect of this: my research capabilities will improve as my tools improve. The improvement rate of those tools can be much greater than my personal improvement rate will ever be - if the tool makers innovate.

Andrew McAfee’s 2019 book More from Less lays out ways in which our tools are evolving to help us get more from less across different sectors of the economy. The core way we have been consistently getting more from less in many industries is because our tools we use in those processes have been improving. May of our tools and technologies have hopped on the Moore’s law curve of exponential improvement.

Surges in human productivity are all about the tools, not the human. What’s cool is that the tool improvements lead to increases in wealth and well being of the human in the loop.

GENERAL PURPOSE TECHNOLOGIES

The emergence of a new set of general purpose technologies (popularly being referred to as AI) promises to accelerate the improvement of our tools — changing the loops we use to do work, and improving the lives of humans.

What is different about general purpose technologies isn’t just that they can be used everywhere. They also tend to be correlated with paradigm shifts. The basic shape of this pattern is that a change in how we think of a problem in one discipline leads to a general purpose technology and then that general purpose technology enables us to think about problems in every discipline in new ways.

As AI is applied to different industries and decision loops, we will not only have to understand the operational and business changes, but we will all likely also have to re-learn things we thought we knew as AI also shifts paradigms or ways of thinking.

We have argued that a paradigm shift is coming in biology, where the inadequacies of machine metaphors and electrical engineering diagrams for cell reactions are obvious. But this is likely to be one of many paradigm shifts. As our tools and paradigms evolve, we will need to evolve with them.

The five minute reel below is extracted from a July 2023 Andrew Ng talk at Stanford in about Opportunities in AI. Despite all the hype around AI and his own obvious optimism about what the technologies can do, Andrew Ng has a discipline that focuses him on teaching the technologies and applying the technologies to concrete use cases. His focus on teaching means that his materials are intended to be understood and applied and useful to us as researchers. His focus on concrete use cases means he is talking about things that are happening and possible now.

CLOSING

AI technologies are already changing how we research companies as the technologies are integrated into the tools we use so it is already irreversibly changing how we do company research.

Because we are generalists and not experts ourselves, we will be learning about AI from experts like Andrew Ng while also learning new AI enabled ways to research companies while the companies themselves will be evolving their processes too.

This is happening — and as it happens there will be gaps between the expectations in prices and the potential of many businesses. So we need to research it and look for those gaps in market expectations and our expectations for various companies.

The process promises to be chaotic and confusing, but full of valuable lessons. Part of me will think of the AI driven chaos, confusion and disruption as a class taught in the style of Richard Feynman. As humans, we need the chaos and confusion because we only learn through struggle. The only way we will learn from our new AI technologies will be to struggle with them.
