A businessman isn’t a mathematician. Remember that. Never become hypnotized by the beauty of numbers. A businessman is someone who buys at ten and is happy to get out at twelve. The other kind of man buys at ten, sees it rise to eighteen and does nothing. He is waiting for it to get to twenty. The beauty of numbers. When it drops to ten again he waits for it to get back to eighteen. When it drops to two he waits for it to get back to ten. Well, it gets back there. But he has wasted a quarter of his life. And all he’s got out of his money is a little mathematical excitement.

                                   V. S. Naipaul, A Bend in the River, p.24

THE NUMBERS TELL A STORY

The abundance of numbers in the financial markets makes it easy for businessmen to mistake themselves for mathematicians and become hypnotized by the beauty of numbers, as Naipaul puts it. One should not ignore the numbers, but the numbers must connect with a narrative or story.  The numbers alone do not mean anything.

The story that can be told with the numbers, that is everything.

MEAN REVERSION IS DEAD, VALUATION ISN’T

The economy evolves and changes.  We all know this – but chasing down what economic changes mean in a way that can inform investment decisions in the present is always a matter of educated guesswork. The structure of the global economy before 1989 was very different than it is now.  In 1989, the Berlin Wall fell and the Eastern Block began to open up to the West, and China continued opening up while clamping down at the same time.  The end of the military confrontation that defined the post WWII period would lead to the commercialization of a host of technologies, including the internet. 

Before 1989, public companies enjoyed a privileged level of permanence within the economy. By undertaking the significant investment required to provide a standard of transparency and governance, they could raise public capital. Furthermore, if they were dealt some blow, their scale and status granted them access to funding that would allow time to work in their favor.  

This literal margin of safety allowed for the process of mean reversion in returns on capital to be probable. Unless mortally wounded, most companies could recover and reward shareholders for their patience. Mean reversion of returns on capital was a key economic phenomenon that generated returns for value investors.

But American capitalism was also transforming in a process described by John Weir Close as  A Giant Cow Tipping by Savages. The emergence of new classes of investors, new financial instruments and new legal theories ended the privilege of corporate survival, and with it the economic bedrock that supported the mean reversion of returns on capital within a corporation over time. 

The time of buying statistically cheap stocks and holding them until someone fixed the problems in a business is over.  Venture investors build new competing businesses from scratch that are not burdened by legacy liabilities or bureaucratic org structures. Distressed debt investors profit from bankruptcy through credit default swaps and make money again as the debt converts to equity, resulting in ownership in an un-levered company with lower costs after bankruptcy.

Profits are made by investing capital to benefit from a stumbling or bumbling incumbent business – in disrupting (venture) or dunking (distress).  The excess returns in venture and distressed investing come from reducing the returns on capital of the incumbent businesses – preventing mean reversion of returns on capital in the legacy business.  This changing ecology of investors and capital markets instruments is one of two key factors which have killed the mean reversion of returns on capital. 

The other factor is the acceleration of technology and innovation. Before the era of rapid technological change, the biggest plant would achieve economies of scale that formed an effective barrier to entry from new capital. In their 2005 book Competition Demystified, Bruce Greenwald and Judd Kahn collapse Porter’s five competitive forces to one: scale.  That worked then. But now technological innovation frequently trumps scale.  We keep getting More from Less as Andrew McAfee describes in his 2019 book.

The newest plants or products have always tended to have the lowest cost structure in an industry. But now the level of that cost advantage is increasingly a function of improvements in technology and process innovations more than as a function of size. Innovation creates stranded assets in legacy businesses because the capital cost of new capacity falls so fast that legacy capital investments cannot be recovered.  Stranded assets are assets that will never earn back the capital invested in them.  These assets revert to zero, not some historic average return.

Value investing is not simply mean reversion in returns on invested capital anymore.  It is “Gretzky at a discount” – businesses skating to where the puck is going to be, while market prices don’t reflect much of an expectation that they can skate.  To have an idea where the puck is going to be, we need a narrative or hypothesis about how different businesses or segments of the economy are evolving.

In the narrative, numbers support a story describing a target market, management team, business franchise, financial model, and expectations in current market prices.  We divide market price changes into shifts in (a) investor expectations and (b) company fundamentals on which those expectations are formed. Investor expectations change more frequently and dramatically than company fundamentals. 

Value investing is about buying low expectations, and valuation is the work of measuring the level of expectations in the price of a security.

CLOSING

One of the greatest risks to an investor is being hypnotized by the numbers which are constantly dancing past us in economic releases, earning releases and price quotes. 

The way to avoid being hypnotized by numbers isn’t more numbers, it’s using the numbers to construct a narrative of how businesses and industries are evolving, in order to get in the way of and profit from changes in the economy. 

Each company has its own story.
