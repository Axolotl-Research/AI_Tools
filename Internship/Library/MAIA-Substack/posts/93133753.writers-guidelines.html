In writing posts for the MAIA substack, team members should use their own voice and writing style, but pieces should be built on some simple rules listed below. These rules will change and evolve and as they do we should update this post.

The overall idea in starting this substack and writing these posts is for us to share our point of view and use the substack to gather feedback and also build a community of people who are interested in some of the same questions around business and investment which we spend our time researching.

A few principles for the style and content of the posts, mostly to keep us on track as we are writing:

  1. Concrete: Based on observations and data.

  1. Relevant: Define economic significance.

  1. Visual: Uses a diagram, picture or graph of some sort.

  1. Linked: Includes footnotes or source links or places to look for additional information.

  1. Conversational: Read it aloud and see how it sounds - aim for a conversational, informal tone.

  1. Concise: If you can “catch” an adjective or adverb, “kill” it (Twain).

  2. Disclose: If we mention an investment security in a post we will disclose if we hold a position in that security. If we mention a company in a post we will not transact in the company in the trading day prior to or after the publication of the post. The trading restriction window is 3 days, including the trading day before and after the publication date.

Maximum length between 1,000 to 2,000 words or 5 to 7 pages with a 10 to 15 minute reading time. Ideally shorter — sub 5 minute reading time.

And just to have a little fun with logic, the last principle or rule:

  1. Realistic: Whenever there are rules, there will be exceptions. Reality is messy and uncertain. Don’t deny that. Messy facts take priority over elegant theories. When facts and theory collide - stick with the facts.

The topics of posts should fit into one or more of the following categories:

  1. Summary or analysis of an industry expert whom we follow.

  2. A note on some part of our investment process - why we do what we do.

  3. An analysis of a issue in the news and how it impacts a company or industry.

  4. Notes from one of our trips, a breif travelogue.

If you have any further questions on writing style, I like the old Kansas City Star copy style sheet from 1917-1918, when Hemingway wrote there. It starts out, “Use short sentences, Use short paragraphs. Use vigorous English. Be positive, not negative.” Hemingway’s style seems to have internalized most of that — the last bit on being positive he obviously strugged with.
