SANDWICHES & STOCKS

"Anyone can cook"

Inspired by Ratatouille, an economist (me - Mario) and a doctor (my brother) embarked on a culinary adventure, creating Pig Brothers, a barbecue restaurant in El Salvador. Starting a business is not easy and it is even more difficult when you are also trying to start a career as a research analyst at the same time.

But just starting takes a lot of responsibility, sacrifice, passion, communication, and empathy. Founders must juggle roles. On Mondays we were the logistics team buying the raw materials, on Wednesdays, we were executive chefs preparing the mise en place, on Fridays, we dressed as salesmen to promote our product throughout the neighborhood and on Sundays we were accountants reporting all expenses and incomes, crossing our fingers that we have enough to pay back our parents for the loan they gave us to start the business.

If you’re ever in San Salvador, don't miss the best-pulled pork sandwich of your life! (shown below)

I didn’t realize starting Pig Brothers would be useful in building my career — my brother and I liked to barbecue, and people liked our barbecue. But a few months after starting work as a junior analyst with the Merchant Adventure team, my boss (Matt) explained that one reason he knew I would be good at the job was because of what I’d learned from starting a business.

At first, I thought he was just being nice. Now I understand that our job is to analyze companies as though we’re the owners, and he knew I’d learned how to think like an owner by starting Pig Brothers.

Whether you own a business or own one share in a company, you can’t understand it unless you think like an owner.

Share Merchant Adventures

When you own a restaurant, you can go to the location, check on your employees, touch your inventory, and entertain the customers. You can touch, smell and feel it. As an owner, you know your business in a way that is much more nuanced than writing a report or building a model.

When I started in the investment world as a junior analyst I learned how to navigate regulatory filings, read financial statements, construct projections, and calculate expectations on the price of a stock or what the price of a stock could be if my projections meant it happened. So it was easy to believe that the feeling of owning this restaurant was divorced from owning a percentage or shares of a public company. I was learning, but these were just the mechanics.

But checking in with my boss, he commented “you built a business from scratch and know the problems that can come with it. Stocks aren’t that different. You know how to do it - just also remember what you are doing. Shares are ownership of a company - so think like an owner of the company.”

When you start thinking like the real owner of the business, the process isn’t mechanical - it’s personal.

Shares are ownership interests, and if you are an owner of a business, you should at least try to think like an owner - even if you are a minority owner. This has some simple but often overlooked implications that jumped out at me.

An owner

  • isn't worried about technical trading patterns, but

  • is worried about growth and profitability. Since my job is to think like an owner and focus on growth and profitability, I realized part of my job was actually to ignore a lot of noise.

So I got started. My piece on thinking like an owner would go in our “Merchant Adventurer’s Guidebook” — functionally a policies and procedures manual for analysts on the team but with a better name than Policies & Procedure Manual. And I realized my boss was doing it again and encouraging me to think like an owner again - this time taking ownership of how we work.

FROM THE GUIDEBOOK:

EXERCISE: IMAGINE YOU ARE RUNNING THE BUSINESS


As a junior analyst, it is difficult to change the analytical/mechanical perspective to a more personal/owner one, but a simple and unstructured exercise to start thinking as an owner is to imagine yourself in the chair of the CEO and start asking yourself questions. You will find that you ask questions differently in this mode and it is often useful in highlighting gaps in our knowledge or understanding of how businesses work or of the elements of a specific business.


A couple of lessons to learn from this exercise:

1. If you understand a company, you'll find that you understand why management is doing things without needing to read their new releases or talk to them about it. If you need to talk to management to understand why they did something, you are still learning the business.

2. If you can't imagine running the business at all, either because something seems crazy to you or it doesn't make sense to you in some way — that is useful analytical information. You need to zero in on exactly what seems crazy or doesn’t make sense, because that is probably where we have something we need to learn. Because, as my boss says: "The devil is in the details."

Closing

When I started analyzing companies, I often gave my opinion from a purely functional point of view. I would report facts and always make sure my facts were correct.

However, as I started thinking like an owner - the facts fell into the background. As an owner I would imagine acting based on today’s facts, and then what that would mean tomorrow. The facts which I had originally thought it was my job to collect were just little slices in a larger story. They mattered, but were just stepping stones. Being able to see the larger story of companies competing and adapting — winning and losing and changing - that was all there.

I thought I was thinking like an owner because as shareholders we are part owners of the businesses we hold shares in. And that was true — but an owner is “in the game.” An analyst can easily fall into the habit of being a critic, but by thinking like owners we were training analysts to think like Roosevelt’s “man in the arena.”

That shift from critic to creator — from thinking like an analyst to thinking like an owner is similar to the shift Brene Brown made for her own reasons as her career took off.

Brene Brown may not be who you expected to see reciting Roosevelt’s famous manly man quote. But any new analyst making the a case to invest millions of dollars on the basis of their research and projections, if they are honest with themselves, gets comfortable with vulnerability. Our financial projections always end up being wrong - the only question is how much?

As analysts we need to imagine we are owners so that we put ourselves into the arena, to face what the future brings and learn from it.
