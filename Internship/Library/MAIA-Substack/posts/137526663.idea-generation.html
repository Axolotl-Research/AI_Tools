There is no magic to investment idea generation — just a little logic and a constant flow of work. The purpose of an idea generation discipline or process is to use simple logical rules to limit the scope and define the order of tasks in reviewing a constant flow of investment opportunities, while also keeping up with all the existing holdings, emerging technologies, and global trends. Logically, idea generation is two parts structured process and one part unstructured imagination.

(Note: Building financial projections for a company is also an imaginative exercise, but it is highly structured and not part of the idea generation process. Idea generation is limited to deciding if a company is worth investing an analyst’s time in. Idea generation is a gating process not an evaluation process.)

UNSTRUCTURED & IMAGINATIVE

The unstructured and imaginative part of idea generation is easily described because there isn’t much that can be said. These are the ideas that come in “over the transom” – from reading, or companies that came up unexpectedly in conversations with competitors or experts. We allocate time and attention to “looking out” for the unexpected in unstructured inquiry. A good analyst is curious about the world in general and this unstructured part of the idea generation process can be thought of as applied curiosity over time.

Since unstructured inquiry doesn’t impose its own limits, time spent in this mode should normally be less than 20% of the time spent doing research.

Our interest in the role of nuclear energy is a recent example of something that knocked around in the unstructured inquiry space as being of interest but without structure for the last year or two, a time during we were just learning little by little as we watched events and read about the companies and the science. During that time, we were able to build enough fundamental knowledge of nuclear energy and merge that with our perspective of future energy demands as well as the companies operating in the space, such that going forward we have a structure for researching developments in nuclear energy and the companies in that space. Over time, themes and companies will emerge from the unstructured space and move into more structured systematic inquiry or they will get discarded.

STRUCTURED & FOCUSED

The structured part of idea generation is one part qualitative: ideas that come up while researching existing holdings (maintenance research) and one part quantitative: ideas that come from security screening. Each of these is discussed in its own section below.

MAINTENANCE RESEARCH

While keeping up with existing holdings or other companies of interest, we listen to quarterly calls and conference presentations; talk to analysts, management teams and experts; read trade journals and books and newspapers. And while we are learning about a subject company, we are also learning about its competitors, and suppliers and clients. Many new ideas come up while keeping up with or refreshing old ideas. There’s not a single way this happens.

Just because you start researching one company doesn’t mean that you will still be researching that company in the end. If you find another company that seems more interesting during your research, that isn’t a mistake or a problem – it means you learned something. That’s the point of research.

New ideas noticed during maintenance research are sort of like motion you see in your peripheral vision. You will be focused on learning about one company – but by focusing on that company, you will also start to see how everything is moving around it and relates to that company. At this point, if you notice that a supplier or competitor or customer has power over the company you are focused on, it should be natural to shift focus to understanding how and why another business has more market power.

SECURITY SCREENING: ROADKILL & EXCELLENT COMPANIES

We utilize two different types of quantitative screens to find new ideas:

1. Roadkill with a pulse:

A significant decline in price (“roadkill”) combined with some set of financial characteristics which indicate some signs of financial life (“pulse”), such that you end up with a list of 100-200 companies. The financial pulse to check for should include some combination of balance sheet and cash flow factors.

Roadkill with a pulse is a phrase we borrowed from John Gunn, retired from Dodge & Cox. The usefulness of this screen is in forcing ourselves to look at companies where things have gone wrong and where the market is discounting a low probability the company will survive let alone succeed. So we check for a pulse.

2. Excellent Company Universe:

Each analyst designs their own screen, producing a list of 100-200 companies with the attractive financial characteristics (“excellent companies”). Each analyst gets to define their own characteristics and can change their criteria for an excellent company any time. Valuation may be included, but shouldn’t be a primary screening factor or constraint. Each analyst must be able to explain the basis for their criteria.

There is no one set of screening criteria for company excellence — leaders in different industries can have dramatically different levels of profitability, asset turnover or financial leverage. The easiest way to think of the trade-offs between characteristics in the excellent company screen is to mentally walk through the DuPont equation.

We borrowed the Excellent Company Universe (“ECU”) from Jim McCluskey, who explained his ECU screen to me when we worked together at SKBA Capital Management in the late 1990’s. As I remember it Jim designed the ECU replicate elements of Buffett’s approach, particularly his attraction to high ROE businesses. But by screening for companies with robust profitability and other signs of financial health, you got a list of companies which were generally the opposite of those on traditional versions of value screens like the Roadkill screen.

Reviewing the ECU list of companies serves two purposes:

  1. If a company from the ECU pops up on Roadkill — it doesn’t tend to stay there. Excellent companies tend to surprise the market with their resilience, and are usually worth taking a closer look at.

  2. Studying companies on the excellent company list keeps analysts more accurately calibrated because if you just look at Roadkill all day every day, you will end up losing track of what a successful company where everything is working looks like.

These two different screening modes deliberately focus on different sets of companies. The Roadkill screen forces our attention to stocks the market is currently punishing the most. We will normally be buying companies that would be showing up on a Roadkill screen. But stocks with significant recent price declines always have problems – either problems that caused the decline or problems that the stock decline caused. As analysts and portfolio managers, it is important that we don’t spend too much of our time looking at problems. If you spend too much time studying problem companies you begin to think problems are normal and forget what a normal company looks like, much less how an excellent company works.

The purpose of the Excellent Company Universe is to allocate time and attention to studying currently successful companies – even if the valuation isn’t attractive. As analysts we are students of excellence in business. Our investment approach is to seek businesses with the seeds of excellence or signs of it, but at a time when the market is focused on their problems. But we can’t recognize these signs of excellence if we aren’t studying it, and if we don’t have an ongoing discussion about what makes an excellent business in different industries.

CLOSING

The inevitable fate of most new ideas is to be shot down - wherever they come from. The point of the process isn’t the ideas, but the lessons learned from constantly scanning the business landscape for disasters, opportunities, emerging themes and excellence.

And, the ultimate goal of all this effort is to learn to recognize excellence in a company at a time when the market doesn’t see it. We are looking for companies that go to eleven, when the market thinks they only go to ten.
