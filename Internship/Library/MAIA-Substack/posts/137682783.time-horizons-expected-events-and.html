Value driver – Growth – Profitability - Adjustments

The ability to build a full model of the projected financial statements of a company is a useful skill to have. There is no better way to get an understanding of how financial statements are linked together and the way money flows through the different financial statements. That said – once one has built a number of these models, you can’t help but notice that the value of the model is in this understanding of the flows and linkages – because the models inevitably fail to predict the future financials. The error rate in the model ends up being proportional to its level of precision.

In forecasting financial expectations our goal is to trade off precision for accuracy. To do this we make a series of simplifying assumptions. Each assumption may seem simple and has the effect of simplifying our analysis and math – but there is solid fundamental basis for each simplifying assumption and none of these things are actually shortcuts – they are mor like tunnels burrowed through rock.

The simplifying assumptions are:

1. Stage 1 - One-Two Years Out: The Sell side Consensus is probably actually fairly accurate for the next 12-18 months, or we aren’t likely to consistently pay more attention to the near future wiggles and wobbles than the street analysts – so the most efficient way to financially model the near future is to use the consensus numbers.

2. Stage 2 – Three – Five Years Out: There may be a midterm growth phase or adjustment phase – a period of normalization between the near-term stuff in the analysts model and the business settling into what we think is a likely long term growth and profitability range.

3. Stage 3: Five Years out – Business has stabilized or we just assume it will have to be able to model it – at a multiple which reflects our expectation of the long-term growth and profitability of the business.

4. We view growth and profitability as being driven either by sales or book/capital. We identify the value driver for the business as either sales or book and then quantify the long term growth as growth in sales or growth in book and long term profitability as net margins or ROE.

5. Using a 15% Target rate of return for all investments, we get a target terminal multiple 5 years out of price to book or price to sales.

6. We allow for adjustments, which usually would involve balance sheet adjustments -- adjusting for cash, stock issuance/buybacks, mergers or spin-offs or debt repayment.

7. We take the value driver at the end of Stage 1, with the analysts concerns about the near term issues baked in, then we make our adjustments, grow or shrink it in an intermediate period (stage 2) and show our math.

8. In its simplest form, the difference between the multiple today and the long-term multiple plus growth in the next 5 years is your return on the investment over 5 years. The return is expected growth plus multiple expansion.

[DRAFT] Scenario Process & Tools

<< also being defined in the application we are working on with Ivan & James.>>

Our scenario process helps us construct detailed event-based expectations for our companies and the markets they operate in. From an academic perspective, we build on the work of Tetlock in Superforecasting. The process is designed as a disciplined way to prepare for the future and how the near future will differ from the recent past. The key analytical errors we are trying to adjust for are expressed by Peter Schwartz in his call for imagination and analytical realism. We must not assume that things will not change and we must be skeptical of boosters breathlessly overstating changes.

Operationally, our scenario process focuses on constructing sets of key future events, structured as yes or no questions (binomial outcomes) over fixed time horizons in different contexts. Mario Orellana and John Magruder are working on constructing an Observable Notebook building off of a Tidy Tree Conditional Probability Notebook created by another Observable user.

Our template will be set up to take as input the following:

1. Type of Scenario

2. Subject

3. Events

4. Order or Path Dependence

5. Future Considerations

The subject is the company or domain/issue which the events in the scenario apply to. The events should be a list of questions framed to fit the type of analysis and including a probability estimate. Functionally, an event is composed of (1) a name, (2) a yes or no question with includes a specific date / deadline, (3) if the event is dependent on another event, path dependence / order, (4) our probability estimate of the event, (5) basis for the probability estimate specifically in terms of either technical feasibility or scaling/distribution issues.

The four types of scenarios:

1. Company Update (Quarterly or other periodic updates)

2. Company Analysis (Investment Thesis)

3. Emerging Economic Potential (Short-term Disruption/Shift)

4. Economic Potential Analysis (Long-term Economic Drivers)

In the case of the first and second types of scenarios, the subject is a company – but the difference between the types is the time horizon under consideration. In the third and fourth type of scenario, the subject could be a country or an industry or the path of a specific technology and the difference is, time horizon. Each type of scenario forces us think expected events in different contexts at different time horizons. We are not predicting the future – but if we can more quickly see what has happened and rapidly discard things that are not possible – well, that’s how one gets an analytical advantage that isn’t based on luck.

The three time horizons for scenarios are:

1. Tactical (12 to 18 months),

2. Strategic (3-5 years),

3. Long-term (7-10 years).

The purpose of the tactical time horizon is to take the key challenges & opportunities we know a company is facing in the near term and frame them in terms of questions for us to watch. The key here isn’t really to get the probabilities right – strangely enough getting the probabilities right only begins to matter as we refine this process over time. The key here is to make sure we can articulate the key opportunities and challenges each of our companies faces in the next 12-18 months and have a framework for tracking their progress. Additionally – once we have events for all the companies, we can also look at the portfolio in terms of weights that are dependent on future events. So the events could function as a forward looking basis for thinking about risks in the portfolio.

The strategic time horizon could be applied to a major economic shift which is under way and which we want to try to detail out more precisely or it would also apply to the construction of the investment thesis for a company. A specific example of a near term economic potential analysis which I think we should put together is the emergence of nuclear power. This is already happening because of Russia’s recent actions and the climate beliefs of the millennials and gen-z. So what events should we be watching for as this gets started? In terms of the strategic company analysis – we should be able to articulate the events which would need to happen for our underlying investment thesis on a company to hold true. So the strategic company analysis should include a set of events pertaining to management, business franchise / financials and valuation which would map to our investment thesis for the company.

The longer-term time horizon only applies to long-term economic potential and general domains of economic opportunity. There is no real granularity to this sort of analysis – but a well-constructed long-term analysis of economic potential should inform what issues we need to dive into and understand in more detail and so while this is less granular and longer term, it is not disconnected from the other time horizons. A long-term view must be consistent with the shorter-term views.

The output of the scenario process is a set of key questions regarding the future of the subject, where the questions are structured around a subject, an event, a time horizon and a probability.  In terms of estimating probabilities, we will need to use common principles with a shared basis so that we can have intelligent discussions about the probabilities. Ultimately, the probabilities are more useful for helping us rank order the likelihood of a series of events than they will be for precisely knowing in any fashion what will happen.  There are three general levels of certainty:

1.     Proximate: It has already started to happen in some way, but could stop (67% or higher)

2.     Probable: all the elements exist for it to happen (33% to 66%)

3.     Possible: could happen but unlikely (less than 32%). 

Thinking about the future is difficult.  Most scenario planning processes are designed to drive strategic decisions about how businesses plan to create change or evolve – scenario planning is designed for actors in the real economy.  But as investors we are not actors or managers, but observers like Drucker’s bystander.  As investors, we can use the overall scenario planning frameworks to mentally prepare ourselves, and structure our own expectations – allowing us to see changes in the economy as they are happening.  Investing is a strange game.  There is a stability to it because individual people, humans, don’t really change – but our technologies, societies and cultures are constantly evolving.  What I am trying to introduce as a structured part of our investment process is the use of scenario thinking to imagine or construct sets of key future events for us to watch for.  These events should be steps in the evolution of business or technology or society from what it is now to something new, and a useful tool in observing progress.
