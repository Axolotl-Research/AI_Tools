“The future is already here, it’s just not evenly distributed.”  -- William Gibson

Businesses make money by mobilizing technological capabilities and human agency – and in the process of doing this they take something that was hard and make it easier, lowering transaction costs and driving human progress.

Businesses don’t do well with fundamental discovery and long-term research.  What businesses do well is scale and breadth – to commodify & distribute innovations and discoveries.  Businesses are the mechanism for distributing the future. And in distributing the future, they often create wealth. 

The purpose of our company research process is to identify exceptional businesses which are characterized by some combination of unique and leading technical capabilities and people – distributing the future and driving human progress.  Our company research framework is outlined below.

1. Business Franchise

                         How does the business take one dollar and turn it into two?

                        What are the technical skills and advantages the company has?

                         Nuts and bolts of the financial model and scaling.

2. Management Quality / Culture

                        Leadership & culture are key to re-investment of capital & organizational resilience. 

                        There are no exceptional results without exceptional people doing exceptional work.

3. Valuation:

                        Gap between business potential and expectations discounted in current price.

                        Napkin math captured in versions of a Gordon Growth Model:

Value = Profits / (Target Return – Growth Rate)

Value (Price / Sales) = Normal Net Margin / (Target Return – Sales Growth Rate)

Value (Price / Book) = Normal ROE / (Target Return – Book Growth Rate)

Perspectives on business franchise, management quality, and valuation combine to form our investment thesis on each company in the portfolio.  Technical capabilities influence the business franchise. Management quality and corporate culture are all about human agency – motivating, engaging, growing, compounding and retaining human intellectual capital.  Valuation is the step of identifying the gap between our expectation and the expectations in the current price, to maximize the probability of profiting from rising expectations (or multiple expansion). 

A NOTE ON VALUATION

We are not trying to create complex models for the valuation thesis, but to imagine a business at scale operating in a steady state.  The purpose of the valuation framework is to get a target price to sales or price to book multiple. 

From a nuts-and-bolts perspective, our target return is 15% and the maximum long-term sales or book growth rate for the purpose of deriving a target valuation multiple is 10%, thus, for any subscale business with long-term growth potential the target multiple to sales = Normal Net Margin / 0.05; or target multiple to book value = Normal ROE / 0.05.  Different businesses are best characterized as being driven either by invested capital producing a return (book value multiple, ROE & book growth framework) or by cranking up sales (sales multiple, net margin, sales growth framework).  Part of knowing a business is knowing which is a better characterization.

Once we really know a business, figuring out the target multiple and our target price becomes the easy part.  This is an important characteristic of a useful valuation discipline – it focuses one on understanding businesses and offers a quick and easy way to compare opportunities and doesn’t get bogged down in technical details of modelling.  Implementations of technically precise models all get precision at the price of understanding the way a business is evolving and being able to compare that precise model to other companies.  This is like Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle applied to investments: you can know exactly what a business is now or where it’s going, but not both.  Investing is about the future, not the past – so we want to know where the businesses are going. 

OBSERVATION & CALIBRATION

Scenario planning and investing are both focused on possible and probable futures, so it makes sense that Peter Schwartz’s description of scenario planning also applies to investing:

“… planning involves imagining different possibilities and then how we can get from here to there.  And so it requires a combination of two things: imagination and analytical realism.”

Most investment operations do analytical realism very well but building a culture that supports imagining an uncertain future and positing scenarios which will certainly be wrong in many ways – well, very few firms do that well.  Our goal is to build a culture which both supports a disciplined analytical process (realism) as well as cultivating a long-term view drawn from imagined paths from here to the future.

All company observations are tested against the investment thesis.  The investment thesis includes a set of factors to watch for or observe to confirm or refute the investment thesis [has to be falsifiable (Popper) and has to watch out for confirmation bias (Cathcart)]. 

Calibration is the modification of the investment thesis based on new information. Decisions to buy or sell are based on the thesis or changes in the thesis. 

All that matters is exactly what we buy and sell, when we do it and where the expectations are in the price at the time of purchase or sale.  All the steps, frameworks, principles, and processes leading to this point are like the gears that deliver force from the engine (the economy) to the wheels (the portfolio).  Buys and sells (trades) are where the rubber meets the road.

The best description of what it is to be an observer is captured in Peter Drucker’s autobiography The Adventures of a Bystander.  As observers, we ask questions and listen to the response.  Observing is a mix of the art of being curious and asking the right questions and being an active listener – hearing and seeing what is there, and not just watching for what one expects.
