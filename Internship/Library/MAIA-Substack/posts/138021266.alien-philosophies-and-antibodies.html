We must defend against foreign ways of thinking – thinking that is not defined in our framework or within our shared language.  We must defend ourselves and our way of thinking with the intensity of Ellen Ripley, and reject alien philospohies like a disease — developing organizational antibodies to reject alien investment philosophies.

This isn’t because ours is the only way of making money, or because we somehow “see the truth” with some vision of “intrinsic value” or other nonsense.  It is because to achieve excellence we need to focus, using our shared imagination and shared language to do more as a team than any of us could as individuals.

Many philosophies can succeeed, but this does not mean that they will work for us. You cannot succeed by trying to be something other than what you are.  A hawk does not decide to try hunting like a cat, nor does a cat try to hunt like a hawk – cats and hawks each hunt in their own way, in the way they have adapted or evolved to hunt.  And both succeed, in their way.

We are not macro investors; we are not short-term traders; we are not merger arbitrage players; we neither buy nor sell volatility as a thing in itself; we do not short; we do not borrow on margin; we have no use for technical analysis; the patterns we see involve profitability, growth and company financials - not security prices.

As Laertes lamented to Hamlet, “there are more things on heaven and earth than are dreamt of in your philosophie.”  We are curious about all those things, but we will always be making sense of them using our terms and frameworks – in terms of business models, management capabilities, valuations and our expectations.
