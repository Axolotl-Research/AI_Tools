Due to complexities of drug pricing, regulations, FDA processes, intellectual capital law and other painful details which have combined to cause the cost of new drug development to increase exponentially over time, biotech companies are currently trading at record low valuations.

In the short-term, this may be justified. Biotech investing is nothing if not risky. Most biotech companies and new drug trials fail.

On the other hand, as our manufacturing, chemistry, computing and sensing or measurement tools get better — industrial and biological processes are moving closer together. The recent book So Simple a Beginning by Raghuveer Parthasarathy elegantly tells that story as well as offering a framework for understanding many of the recent advances in biotech.

Biomimicry isn’t just a macro scale phenomenon and process shortcut. As we are able to examine biological processes in greater detail we will inevitably discover more efficient methods for industrial processes. Biological methods of fixing nitrogen used by diatoms may be more effective than the Haber-Bosch process in certain ways — we won’t know until we’ve figured out how the diatoms do it. But we have the knowledge and tools to ask and test these questions now.

These aren't just intellectual thought experiments. As our understanding of biology improves, becoming more detailed and granular, this won't just offer improvements in human health but will also yield innovations across material science and industrial processes.  For me, listening to Rob Philips talks on YouTube during the pandemic drove this point home — particularly his talks on Molecular Vitalism and Allostery.

Philips’ description of high fidelity polymerization / DNA replication, shown below as an excerpt from his talk on Molecular Vitalism at the Kavli Institute, is one of the many examples in his talks which left me in awe of the potential of biological processes.

Closing

There are more concrete ways to explain the potential of biological discoveries to improve both human health and industrial efficiency. There’s an alphabet soup of technologies and techniques one could use to explain the sector, or financial explanations of generalist investors and specialist investors and the current status of major pharma R&D pipelines and balance sheets could be explored and analyzed. The details always matter.

But if we get stuck in the details and how biotech has worked or not worked over time, I think we miss the scale of what is going on. We risk ending up like Agent Kujan from the Usual Suspects, and although all the pieces are there in front of us — we don’t step back and look at them until its too late.

It all makes sense when you step back. There is an order to biology which we are just beginning to learn to see and manipulate.

Perspective and point of view matter. Our expectations or initial assumptions limit and define our conclusions in ways we often miss. The only way not to fall into the trap that caught Agent Kujan is to be curious and look around with wonder, like a child. To follow one question to the next question rather than trying to rush to an answer or look for confirmation of your beliefs.

The discoveries tha\t will come from a better understanding of biology — which is a highly precise process, and the opposite of sloppy, are likely to be more significant than we can imagine. We are on the cusp of a paradigm shift which we will discuss more in later posts bringing in Dan Nicholson's process focused perspective.

In fact, we would argue that what is working with AI systems is mimicking the way cortical columns in the neocortex construct what Jeff Hawkins refers to as reference frames. Large Language Models (LLM’s) construct vectors or tensors, which appear to be logically analogous to the reference frames in the cortical columns of the neocortex. Our technologies and our biology are stumbling toward each other, and the fruits of that process will change everything.
