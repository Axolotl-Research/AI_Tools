Graham & Dodd opened Security Analysis with this quote from Horace, which can be read in different ways. For Horace, who served as an officer in the Republican army that Mark Antony crushed at Philippi and then became a spokesman for Augustus and leading poet of Rome - the idea of being fallen and returned to honor must have resonated. The idea wasn’t some abstraction or ideal of a poet — it described the harsh reality of his life. For Graham, who made his name first as a growth stock investor in the 1920’s before his own fall in the crash of 1929, and re-invention as a value investor par excellence — it must have resonated as well. The markets are neither kind, nor constant.

This is a sort of lesson or sentiment that comes with a certain age. One can’t live a long life without seeing different cycles, different seasons. As the American poet Robert Frost observed, nothing gold can stay.

Interest Rates

We have been through a remarkable period of time over the last several years. It’s hard to say when it all began, but we will start with eight years of near zero interest rates from 2009 to 2017, a period that included leading central banks introducing negative interest rates. Then we had the pandemic and we returned to near zero rates, for a time before an inflationary cycle finally started.

Peter Bernholz noted in his book that the build up of inflation can take a long time. Basically, you have to wait until people either don’t believe inflation is possible, or governments are so desperate they don’t care, or both.

But it is still unclear if this inflation is the result of bizarre pandemic shifts from a services economy to a locked-down goods economy and now back to a services economy — or if the shocks of the pandemic and extraordinary government stimulus shook something else loose. It looks to us like the inflation we have been experiencing is more of the former than the latter, although the fiscal stimulus in the US continues even if the monetary stimulus has ended.

Fiscal and monetary policy impact and react to each other but they are not coordinated. If this makes the economy sound like a car with two steering wheels — well that’s reasonably accurate…. as long as you realize that since the links between the steering and the wheels are unknown and indeterminate and change over time based on the context.

Perhaps the most shocking thing about the US interest rate environment is that no one younger than 40 years old has any meaningful experience with interest rates measurably above zero.

One needs to note that during the period of low rates, regulation and politics were not the answer. The regulatory programs formulated in response to crises has tended to entrench incumbents and make the economy more brittle — planting the seeds for the next crisis.

The research and discussions around the role of regulation in the economy are a fetid swamp. The best way to describe this may be to just note that there are no independent experts on anything. The process of developing expertise over time builds a network of relationships and leads one to develop a specific point of view. The expert sees few possibilities because they have reached the point of thinking they know things which were true but may not be true anymore.

The only thing that we know for certain about regulation is that it sets the rules for economic activity. Economies tend to run best when people know what to expect and how to plan.  Any change in regulation has a re-planning or uncertainty cost - it is not as simple as reducing or increasing regulation. The key to effective regulation is stability of expectations. 

Without stable expectations how can you have stable prices? And if we live in a world where government is active, or where an election can swing regulatory policy in dramatic fashion — this is a cost, and a problem.

But the problem isn’t too much regulation, or too little regulation. The long-term problem is with inconstant and shifting regulations which make planning more expensive and uncertain.

Valuations

The pandemic clearly shook something loose in terms of stock market expectations as the market’s briefly valued 40% of the S&P 500 at over 8x sales. Remember the S&P 500 is made up of very large companies and to justify an 8x price-sales ratio the market is expecting that the company will have both extraordinary growth and extraordinary margins in perpetuity with limited need for re-investment to defend it’s franchise or innovate. No large business actually works that way except those who are collecting royalties on patents or other intellectual property — but even those are exposed to new inventions, so they aren’t perpetuities.

During the pandemic, the market effectively developed its own sort of cabin fever. But, that season seemed to be ending, just like the season of low interest rates. Then the cabin fever came back.

For one to win in the market over the last few years, one needed to suspend disbelief as well as stop doing the basic math on valuations. Higher interest rates have a way of forcing investors to do the math and analyze their opportunity costs. Ultimately, Herb Stein’s law is likely to apply to valuations as it must -- things that can’t go on forever do eventually end.

To drive this point home, very few large businesses can deliver the combination of growth and profitability to justify a valuation over 4x sales. But most of the S&P 500 is expected to be able to accomplish this at current prices.

So the current market seems to believe in a place even better than Lake Wobegon, where all the children (and companies) were above average. Within the S&P 500, the median company gets an exceptional valuation. It’s like a class in which most of the grades are A’s. In that sort of classroom we might conclude the teacher (or Mr. Market) had become a very easy grader. Easy grading is the kind of thing that builds with free money — but it may fade in a world where money has a real cost.

What’s Next?

Which leads us to the question that Horace had in mind as he wrote his advice to young Romans: how to prepare for what’s next?

The seasons have a predictable turn and rhythm of change that life lacks. Markets follow life’s unpredictability and are driven by surprises. rather than any sort of seasonal or cyclical pattern. Markets are driven by surprises, not systemic factors – although surprises do occur regularly such that a quantitative analyst can be forgiven for conflating persistent surprises with something systematic.

The central role of surprises in markets is why Graham & Dodd opened with Horace. We don’t know what’s next, and don’t think anyone really does. That’s what it means for surprises to be the driver of the market and systematic.

But, as William Gibson observed, “the future isn’t even distributed.” It arrives in one place and is distributed and scaled through businesses using capital and labor. So, while we don’t know what’s next, we can observe what has already happened but hasn’t scaled or spread yet.

So where might we look for that? What’s next or what’s next to scale? How can one invest in companies driving that change at a reasonable valuation?

These are the real questions, which we are just dancing around in this post. There’s no precise answer for how to find what’s next, just an endless series of questions.

During the pandemic , we witnessed an amazing episode of convergent discovery: many different groups inventing vaccines for a type of virus which we hadn’t ever invented a vaccines for. Now the market values that biotechnology discovery complex at the lowest levels in over 20 years. This is happening at a time when developed world median ages are marching past 50 in many places - an age at which maladies begin to expand at an exponential rate.

Arguably biotech valuations are falling because the cost of developing new drugs keeps rising, and the probabilities of success seem to have gone from low to lower.  Dylan Patel at Semianalysis wrote an excellent data rich post playing with Moore’s Law and it’s opposite.  Biotech seems to be doing the opposite Moore’s Law right now, Eroom’s Law. We don’t think this will continue — but nothing in the market gets neglected without a reason.

The chart below shows that on a price to book basis, the S&P 500 Biotech Index trades at a significant discount to the S&P Healthcare Index, which trades for about the same valuation level as the S&P 500 overall. The valuation of health care innovation being less than healthcare which is less than or at about the same level of the overall market is odd: biotech supplies health care innovation and health care demand growth should exceed the growth of the economy as the developed world ages.

The historical order of valuations has been reversed because biotech has been trapped by a rising cost curve. The industry has to find a way to escape Eroom’s law and hop onto Moore’s Law. This may be what AI does for biotech — helps it find a way to finally turning advances in technology into declining costs as well as life saving innovations. Or it may be that someone makes lab equipment as easy to use as an iPhone — or a combination or re-combination of both. Innovation is more about re-combinations than discovery. The details of drug discovery’s escape from Eroom’s Law will its own series of posts over time.

Biotech has amazing capabilities, is increasingly in demand as people age, and is priced with the lowest expectations in a generation. The low expectations are based on a real phenomenon which has been given it’s own name as an economic law, Eroom’s law. We don’t think biotech will be trapped in Eroom’s law forever, and we think we are seeing signs of how companies may escape. One way to think about AI and Biotech, for instance, would be that the impact of AI may be to help provide tools to finally break the drug discovery process out of an increasing cost curve and onto a decreasing cost curve.

What we look for in researching businesses is precisely this sort of mismatch between the potential of a business and the expectations in current prices. The gap between economic potential and expectations in market prices is what an investment opportunity looks like. We refer to this mismatch as “progress when it’s not in the price.”

[Disclaimer: Graphs of valuation levels of different indices are useful general guides, but indices are made up of companies — and although one can invest in indices, if one doesn’t understand the underlying companies in an index, you don’t actually know anything.

The point here is that no one can claim to know something abstractly — whenever you get to know something it is by getting into the details. In the world of investing, this means understanding companies and not ever stopping your analysis at the index level. These observations on index valuation levels aren’t an answer or recommendation of any sort. They are an invitation to ask more questions. These graphs lead me to my own questions — but for the reader, the key is what questions they lead you to ask and how would you go about researching those questions? Looking for answers but knowing that the best questions just lead to more questions. This is the game in company research, economic research, scientific research, etc.]

We never know what’s next, but we can pay close attention to what has already happened and what is expected in market prices and look for gaps. We would argue the gap between the potential of biotech and the expectations for biotech companies right now is one of the most interesting parts of the market.

The current excitement about AI is also a useful example of economic potential, but one where the innovation is expected to produce exceptional profits and the companies driving AI innovation are priced to reflect this. There is no gap. Or that’s what it looks like — but the market will surprise us. In the long-term, the only safe bet is probably that, as Horace and Graham and Dodd all noted, fortune is characterized by reversals and fortunes can be made and lost in those reversals.

Nothing gold will stay.
