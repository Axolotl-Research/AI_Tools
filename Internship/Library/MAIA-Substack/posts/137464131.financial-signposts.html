One can easily get lost in all the financial ratios and indicators that different practitioners promote. Data that should function as signposts directing you through your analysis of a company often promote chaos and confusion.

Our goal is to try to boil all the complexity down to simple frameworks.  This doesn’t get rid of the complexity – but it offers us a way to contain it, the same way that a line item in an accounting statement contains a mess of journal entries but can be understood without needing to worry about the journal entries.

In our valuation work, everything rolls up to measures of profitability and growth.  This brief note is intended to introduce a few financial statement indicators of market power and management skill.  The idea being that without market power and management skill it is difficult to have much confidence in the ability of a business to deliver financial performance over time.  So finding indicators of market power and management skill in a companies financial statements is a useful check or confirmation of a company’s capability of performing.

MARKET POWER

There are two classic signs of market power that are part of every set of financial statements:

1.     Gross Margin Stability: a company which can maintain a stability of gross margins, or is able to hold steady the mark-up they get on a product over their cost to make the product (cost of good sold) displays a combination of discipline and market power.  Long-term market power is stability through an economic cycle or a period including a strong economy and a weak economy.  Market power is not the ability to increase gross margins, since that’s something customers will notice and won’t appreciate in the long run.

2.     Negative Working Capital: If a company can require its customers to pay it for goods quickly while it can also negotiate with suppliers to give it a long time to pay, the company has market power.  Quantitatively this shows up as days payable outstanding minus days sales outstanding being a large positive number.

Market power is a sign of skill but it is more complicated than just management skill, because in the case of market power we are observing a more complex phenomenon or set of circumstances that enables a business to be able to charge and hold steady there mark-up on their product through economic cycles, or where their customers are happy to pay quickly and their suppliers will settle for being paid later. Power is this ability to dictate price and to dictate payment timing and terms. 

There are other factors that are arguably signs of market power – but these two are the main ones.  The only tricky factor is that everyone knows these metrics are being watched, so you also must learn to look for how companies play games.  That will take you into the creative shenanigans accounting literature and the works of Shilit, Mulford and Comiskey.

MANAGEMENT SKILL

The way I quantify management skill is similar to the framework of Raef Raesendes and Dan Obrycki of the Applied Finance Group (“AFG”).  A lot of the assumptions and techniques in AFG’s Economic Margin Framework are the best practices accounting adjustments to get at the underlying economics of the business.  Then they add some other fancy stuff too – but all the basics are sound.

LONG-TERM CAPITAL ALLOCATION

The concept that I borrow from AFG is that the CEO’s most important job is long-term capital allocation and you can score the CEO based on long-term capital allocation.  The CEO’s job in allocating capital is to drain capital from under-performing businesses and allocate additional capital to outperforming businesses.  So the CEO is doing a good job if they are decreasing capital and returns are steady or improving, or if they are increasing capital and returns are improving.  The no-no is increasing capital and decreasing returns. 

In terms of our focus on rapid progress, under-performing businesses are likely past the steep part of their diminishing return curve and outperforming businesses are probably on the steep part of their curve. So the long-term capital allocation process is where businesses are hopping from diminishing return curve to diminishing return curve.

Notice I haven’t specified the time horizon over which to measure change in capital and change in returns on capital?  Yeah – that’s the difficult part with this measure.  So it’s not quite as simple as just running a financial model of a point in time, but you can graph the returns on capital and changes in invested capital over time and when you know a business you can walk through the story it tells and that understanding is a useful perspective on how the CEO has performed in allocating long-term capital.

The reinvestment of profits and reallocation of long-term capital toward areas of higher return are essential to the success of every business.  If a company has a return on equity of 20%, that means that in the next five years the CEO could reinvest or dividend out more in profits than the current equity capital of the business.  The way those profits are allocated will be a key driver of the business.  And how profits are reinvested and long-term capital allocated is one of the CEO’s key responsibilities.

As analysts, we might not be able to have a precise quantitative scorecard for the CEO, but we can get an EKG of sorts by looking at a long time series of relevant data.

SHORT-TERM CAPITAL ALLOCATION

While the CEO is focused on the long-term capital cycle, the CFO’s daily job includes the way the company breathes cash in and out of its accounts.  The CFO’s scorecard is the short-term or the working capital cycle.

While negative working capital is a sign of market power – negative working capital alone isn’t a sign of CFO skill.  The structure or amount of working capital that a business may need to run efficiently isn’t at the discretion of the CFO. 

The CFO’s job is to run the business he has as efficiently as possible. A CFO gets scored on the stability or consistent incremental improvement of the working capital accounts over time.  An exceptional CFO will maintain steady working capital accounts through different economic conditions, and you will see signs of incremental efficiencies in some of the numbers.  But the key is the ability to maintain consistency. Stable working capital accounts in the face of shifting market environments requires exceptional discipline and skill.

The relationship of the working capital accounts is best visualized as a time series graph converting each account to “days balances” so the that graph is showing you a picture of the timing of how cash goes through the working capital accounts: payables, inventory, receivables, cash.

FUTURE WORK

We have old screens and templates for looking at these items, but a good task for an intern or junior analyst would be to start with a blank sheet of paper and think about how they would create tools to visualize or evaluate these and other financial signposts.

The process of building visualizations for financial ratios and metrics over time is a wonderful way to begin to link the lines in a chart with events or decisions driving a business.
