Plenty of ink has been spilled explaining how to analyze companies.  The fact of the matter is that each company is idiosyncratic – or its success is likely to be idiosyncratic.  Company analysis is a sort of reverse Anna Karenina problem: “Unsuccessful companies are all alike; every successful company is successful in its own way.”

The task of analyzing a company is not to find the ways in which it fits some specific form, but the process of understanding how it functions and what elements “make it successful in its own way” – in a way that is different. Then map those differences to their financial consequences in terms of either profitability or growth.

Note, we are trying to say what an exceptional company looks like – but the key is that an exceptional company in a specific business will be the only one of its kind. 

Analyzing an exceptional company is a process of getting to know and understand the company by studying it over time.  It doesn’t fit a single form; it is best described as an adventure.

WHERE TO START

Company research should always start with how the company sees themselves. Reading company filings and reports and listening to presentations. We all involuntarily anchor on where we start our work, and if we are going to anchor on something the most useful anchor in analyzing a company is how it describes itself.

It is important not to begin with sell side or media reports. Even though those may be excellent sources and correct - they aren’t reliable anchors.

PUTTING IT DOWN & PICKING IT BACK UP

Most companies won’t make sense the first time you look at them, and when you get to something that doesn’t make sense – then you put it down.  Being able to spend some time on a company and learn a little bit and then move on is its own skill.

But just because we have looked at something before and passed on investing doesn’t mean we have rejected it permanently.  If a company name keeps coming up or you keep coming across information on a company – you want to pick it back up and take another look and learn a little more.

This cycle of picking up and putting back down and picking up again can go on every few months for years in some cases.  This is an excellent way to learn about a company, because one of the most difficult things to learn about a business is how it responds to challenges and changes over time.

The only way to really learn about a business as an outside analyst is to study it over time, and the best way to do this is probably not to spend a brief period of intense study, but through and extended period of intermittent pieces of work.

Each time an analyst picks up a business to look at it, the output should be research that captures one important aspect of the business which can be shared with the team. Over time the pieces tend to come together, and in the process each analyst is learning and sharing what they learn as well.

SEEING WHAT ISN’T THERE YET

Ultimately, the time for us to start building a position is when we begin to be able to see what isn’t on the page yet or what hasn’t happened yet.  Understanding an existing business is simple – but to be able to see the potential of a management team and the business they are running takes focus and imagination.

The easiest way to think of this is that at some point an analyst reaches a critical point in their knowledge of a company and starts to see something like a chessboard of moves and is able to articulate not just what the company is but also where it wants to go, how its trying to get there and how it might respond to different challenges or opportunities.

FOXES & HEDGEHOGS

Getting to the point or summarizing research findings isn’t as easy as it would seem. The analysts job is to think like a fox noticing and gathering all kinds of detailed information, and then to learn to flip a switch, suddenly seeing like a hedgehog and formulate a clear concise investment case. A good analyst probably shares the quality Isaiah Berlin loved in Tolstoy, that he “was by nature a fox, but believed in being a hedgehog.”

Writing up a company once we think we understand what makes it exceptional and think we see what isn’t there yet is a matter of finding a way to tell the story of the investment opportunity as simply as possible in terms of the company’s business, management and valuation. Taking what the fox saw and explaining it terms the hedgehog understands.

Luckily, since hedgehogs are simple — the basic story always has the same arc even though the details will be idiosyncratic. The story goes something like this:

  1. Business: The company has these characteristics which enable it to accomplish Y, some of these characteristics are exceptional and difficult to copy.

  2. Management: The CFO is paying attention to working capital and the CEO is making strategic decisions and they are planning on achieving Y.

  3. Valuation: The market expects X in terms of future growth and profitability for the company. We expect Y, and Y is greater than X.

CLOSING

In Vonnegut’s terms from his classic shapes of stories, value investing can generally be thought of as a version of “company in a hole” which is a version of the “man in hole” story shape. But sometimes it’s not a man in a hole, but a little girl, in which case it’s a Cinderella story.

For our purposes, the hole the protagonists start in is the gap between the expectations of the market and the potential of the business.
